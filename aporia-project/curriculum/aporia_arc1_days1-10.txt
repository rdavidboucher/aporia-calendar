Title: The Retreat That Is Always Available
Thinker: Marcus Aurelius
Arc: 1 | Day: 1 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"People try to get away from it all — to the country, to the beach, to the
mountains. You always wish that you could too. Which is idiotic: you can get
away from it anytime you like. By going within. Nowhere you can go is more
peaceful — more free of interruptions — than your own soul."
(Meditations 4.3, trans. Gregory Hays)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Marcus Aurelius wrote this in the second century AD while running an empire —
managing wars on the frontier, presiding over plague, arbitrating Senate
disputes. He was the most powerful man alive, and he could not leave. The
passage is not a platitude about meditation. It is a strategic observation from
a man who had every external retreat available to him and discovered that none
of them worked.

The claim is specific: external retreat — the vacation, the silent weekend, the
phone-free morning — only provides temporary relief because you bring your mind
with you. The beach does not quiet the mind. The mind quiets the mind. And the
route to that quieting is available at any moment, not just when you can afford
the flight.

What Aurelius is pointing at is the discipline of prosoche — the Stoic practice
of self-attention, of watching your own thinking rather than being carried by it.
This is the founding practice of the entire arc you are beginning. Everything
that follows — flow, beginner's mind, deep work, deliberate living — is a
variation on this one observation: the quality of your life is downstream of
the quality of your inner attention, and that attention is trainable.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Today, once before noon, when you feel the pull toward a distraction — the
phone, the news feed, the unnecessary check — stop for sixty seconds before
acting on it. Do not suppress the impulse. Simply observe it. Ask: what am I
actually trying to get away from right now? Name it. Write it in one sentence.
Then decide whether to act on the distraction or return. The decision matters
less than the observation.

--- THE TAGS ---

[mindset] [practice] [internally focused] [overlap]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Research on the default mode network (Buckner et al., 2008) suggests that the
mind's resting state is not passive but actively generative — self-referential
thinking, rumination, and future planning dominate when external focus is
removed. This directionally supports Aurelius's claim that "going within"
requires active attention, not mere withdrawal from external stimuli.
[Qualifier: The Stoic concept of prosoche is a trained philosophical practice
with ethical dimensions that exceed what neuroscience's description of
resting-state cognition captures.] (Good directional support)

--- SRS REVIEW ---

None. (Day 1 — no prior material.)

--- TODAY'S PROMPT ---

Archetype: Synthetic

what is the "beach" you keep planning to escape to? What do you believe will
finally allow you to focus? Write for 3 minutes on what you are actually waiting
for before your real life begins.

Title: The Surgeon's Field
Thinker: Epictetus
Arc: 1 | Day: 2 | Prompt Archetype: Audit

--- THE SEED ---

"Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about
the things: for example, death is nothing terrible, for if it were, it would
have seemed so to Socrates; for the opinion about death, that it is terrible,
is the terrible thing."
(Enchiridion 5, trans. Thomas Wentworth Higginson)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Epictetus was a slave. Not metaphorically — legally. He had no property, no
political standing, and at some point his master broke his leg to demonstrate
ownership of his body. He went on to found one of the most influential
philosophical schools in the ancient world and was cited by Marcus Aurelius —
who was emperor — as a primary teacher. The biographical gap between their
social positions and their shared conclusions is itself a philosophical argument.

The dichotomy of control — the founding move of Epictetan Stoicism — is simple
to state and nearly impossible to fully practice: some things are "up to us"
(our judgments, impulses, desires, aversions) and some things are "not up to
us" (our bodies, reputations, offices, and everything external). The Stoics
called the first category ta eph' hemin and the second ta ouk eph' hemin. Every
moment of misplaced attention — every minute spent on the second category while
believing you are working on the first — is wasted attention.

What makes this hard in practice is that the boundary is not always where you
think it is. Your reputation feels like it should be in the first category. Your
health, your relationship, your project's reception — all of these feel
controllable. Epictetus's point is that confusing the two categories is not a
minor error. It is the source of most human disturbance. The surgeon's field —
the small area where you can actually cut — is much smaller than you assume.
But working precisely within it is how things actually get done.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Before you begin work today, write two columns: "In my field" and "Outside my
field." Take one current project or problem you are carrying. List every element
of it. Sort each element into the correct column honestly. Your quality of work
goes in column one. The reception of that work goes in column two. Then: close
or ignore column two for the rest of the day.

--- THE TAGS ---

[mindset] [practice] [internally focused] [warning]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Research on perceived control and performance (Rotter, 1966 — internal vs.
external locus of control; Langer, 1975) directionally supports the Epictetan
claim that individuals who attribute outcomes to their own actions show lower
anxiety and higher persistence. [Qualifier: The Stoic dichotomy is a
metaphysical claim about the nature of agency, not merely a psychological
reframe. Locus of control research measures subjective perception, not the
underlying ontological distinction Epictetus is drawing.] (Good directional
support)

--- SRS REVIEW ---

None. (Day 2 — insufficient prior material.)

--- TODAY'S PROMPT ---

Archetype: Audit

Think about the last time you were genuinely anxious or frustrated about
something. Looking at it now through the two-column lens: how much of your
attention was aimed at the "outside my field" column? What would you have done
differently if you had caught the misallocation in real time? Be specific —
name the situation.

Title: The Experiment of Living
Thinker: Henry David Thoreau
Arc: 1 | Day: 3 | Prompt Archetype: In-The-Wild

--- THE SEED ---

"I went to the woods because I wished to live deliberately, to front only the
essential facts of life, and see if I could not learn what it had to teach, and
not, when I came to die, discover that I had not lived."
(Walden, "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For")

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Thoreau went to Walden Pond on July 4, 1845 — the choice of date almost
certainly intentional, a personal declaration of independence. He stayed two
years, two months, and two days. He also walked to his mother's house for meals
more frequently than Walden implies, a biographical gap worth naming directly:
the lived practice did not fully match the written prescription. This is not an
indictment of Thoreau. It is actually the most honest thing about him. He was
conducting an experiment, not reporting a completed result. The experiment was:
what happens if you deliberately strip back the non-essential and attend only to
what remains?

The word "deliberately" is doing the most work in that sentence. Walden is not
an argument for poverty or isolation. It is an argument against unconscious
living — against the condition Thoreau describes elsewhere as "lives of quiet
desperation," where the accumulation of obligations, possessions, and busyness
proceeds automatically, without anyone deciding it should. The woods were the
controlled condition of the experiment. The finding was not "live in the woods."
The finding was: most of what you think you need is covering something you have
never looked at directly.

The "essential facts of life" Thoreau wanted to front are still the same facts.
They did not change between 1845 and now. What changed is the volume and speed
of the cover.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Today, identify one recurring obligation in your week that you did not
consciously choose — it accumulated, was inherited, or you simply never
questioned it. Do not eliminate it today. Simply write one sentence: "I have
this obligation because ___." Fill the blank honestly. If you cannot fill it,
that is the answer.

--- THE TAGS ---

[practice] [mindset] [internally focused] [warning]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Research on decision fatigue (Baumeister et al., 1998; Hagger et al., 2010 —
though the ego depletion model has significant replication issues post-2015)
directionally supports the Thoreauvian claim that the proliferation of
unnecessary choices and obligations drains the cognitive resources available for
deliberate attention. [Qualifier: Thoreau's claim is normative — about how life
should be organized — while the ego depletion research is descriptive and
mechanistic. The replication issues in this literature are substantial; treat as
speculative application.] (Speculative application)

--- SRS REVIEW ---

None. (Day 3 — insufficient prior material.)

--- TODAY'S PROMPT ---

Archetype: In-The-Wild

Look at your calendar for this week. Thoreau's question, applied directly: which
blocks of time did you choose deliberately, and which accumulated without
decision? Pick the one block you feel most ambivalent about. Write for 3
minutes: if Thoreau were auditing your week, what would he circle?

Title: The State You Are Already Chasing
Thinker: Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi
Arc: 1 | Day: 4 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"The best moments in our lives are not the passive, receptive, relaxing times...
The best moments usually occur when a person's body or mind is stretched to its
limits in a voluntary effort to accomplish something difficult and worthwhile."
(Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience, Chapter 1 — paraphrase of core
concept; not a verbatim extract)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Csikszentmihalyi spent decades asking people when they felt most alive. Not
happiest in a hedonic sense — not most comfortable or most relaxed — but most
fully present, most engaged, most themselves. The answer was consistent across
cultures, professions, and ages: during challenging activity requiring full
attention. Surgeons in difficult operations. Chess players in deep games. Rock
climbers on hard routes. Mothers fully absorbed in play with a young child. The
state he named "flow" is not exotic. It is the phenomenological description of
what perfect attention feels like from the inside.

The structure of flow is precise: the difficulty of the task must be calibrated
to the skill of the practitioner. Too easy and attention drifts into boredom.
Too hard and it collapses into anxiety. The channel between them — where
challenge and skill are simultaneously at their edge — is where the self
temporarily disappears. Not in the sense of dissolution, but in the sense that
the usual noise of self-monitoring and self-consciousness goes quiet. You are
the action. Nothing is left over to be distracted.

What connects this to Aurelius (Day 1) and Epictetus (Day 2) is a finding the
Stoics would have recognized: flow is not achieved by seeking it. It is the
byproduct of attending fully to the right difficulty. You cannot decide to enter
flow. You can only create the conditions — the calibrated challenge, the removed
distraction, the skill brought to its edge — and then pay attention.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Today, identify the one task you have been avoiding that is at or slightly beyond
your current skill level — not the comfortable task, not the overwhelming one.
The one where you know, if you sat down fully, you could probably do it but it
would require something from you. Schedule exactly 90 minutes for it. No
browser. No phone in the room. Set a visible timer. When it ends, do not measure
output. Measure only: did you stay with the difficulty?

--- THE TAGS ---

[mindset] [practice] [internally focused] [evidence-based]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Csikszentmihalyi's flow research has been replicated across multiple cultures
and methodologies using the Experience Sampling Method (ESM) with good
consistency. The challenge-skill balance model has directional support from
research on dopamine and task engagement (Schultz, 1997). [Qualifier: Flow is a
phenomenological concept derived from self-report data. The neuroscientific
correlates are suggestive but the causal chain from dopamine systems to
subjective flow experience is not fully established.] (Strong alignment for
behavioral findings; speculative application for the neural mechanism)

Title: What You Bring to the Door
Thinker: Shunryu Suzuki
Arc: 1 | Day: 5 | Prompt Archetype: Audit

--- THE SEED ---

"In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind
there are few."
(Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind, Introduction)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Suzuki Roshi did not mean this as an argument against expertise. He meant it as
a description of what expertise tends to do to attention if it is not carefully
watched. The expert walks into a room and sees what they already know how to
see. The category is formed before the door opens. The beginner walks in
genuinely not knowing — which is uncomfortable, slightly vulnerable, and also
the only state in which something genuinely new can enter.

The Zen tradition calls this shoshin — beginner's mind, or initial mind. It is
the quality of attention that a beginner brings involuntarily (because they have
no choice) and that an expert must cultivate deliberately (because their
patterns work and there is no obvious reason to question them). Suzuki is making
a claim about what closes off: not ignorance, not failure, but the accumulated
weight of competence. The more you know how to do something, the more invisible
your assumptions become. They are not checked because they have always worked.

The connection to Aurelius's prosoche (Day 1) is direct: both are about watching
your own mental activity rather than being carried by it. The difference is that
Aurelius is watching for disturbance and distraction; Suzuki is watching for the
more subtle problem — the closed pattern that looks like knowledge but has
stopped seeing.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Today, pick one domain where you consider yourself competent — a skill, a
relationship, a professional area. Before you engage with it, write three things
you do not actually know about it but have been assuming. Not things you are
ignorant of — things you have been taking for granted. Approach that domain
today as if one of your three assumptions might be wrong.

--- THE TAGS ---

[mindset] [practice] [internally focused] [overlap]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Research on expert-induced blindness and functional fixedness (Duncker, 1945;
Wiley, 1998) directionally supports the Zen claim that expertise constrains the
range of solutions a practitioner considers. More recent work on creativity and
knowledge (Dane, 2010, Academy of Management Review) suggests expertise
increases deep within-domain problem-solving but decreases the ability to
generate novel cross-domain solutions. [Qualifier: Suzuki's beginner's mind is
a contemplative practice with ethical and existential dimensions that a cognitive
psychology study on functional fixedness does not capture.] (Good directional
support)

Title: The Tax on Every Interruption
Thinker: Cal Newport
Arc: 1 | Day: 6 | Prompt Archetype: In-The-Wild

--- THE SEED ---

"A deep life is a good life. The ability to focus without distraction on a
cognitively demanding task is becoming both increasingly rare and increasingly
valuable."
(Deep Work — paraphrase of core thesis; not a verbatim extract)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Newport is not making a productivity argument, though it is often read as one.
The deeper claim in Deep Work is phenomenological, closer to Csikszentmihalyi
than to any time-management system: the kind of work that requires your full
cognitive attention is also the kind of work that feels most meaningful while
you are doing it and most satisfying in retrospect. Shallow work — email,
meetings, reactive tasks — expands to fill whatever time you give it and leaves
no felt residue of accomplishment.

The specific mechanism Newport identified that most productivity literature
ignores is "attention residue" — a term from research by Sophie Leroy (2009).
When you switch from Task A to Task B, part of your cognitive attention remains
on Task A, degrading performance on Task B. This is not a character flaw or a
focus problem. It is a structural feature of how cognitive switching works.
Every interruption carries a hidden tax that extends well beyond the interruption
itself. A two-minute check of email at the wrong moment does not cost two
minutes — it costs the cognitive depth of the following twenty.

The argument connects directly to Thoreau (Day 3): just as Thoreau argued that
most obligations are accepted unconsciously, Newport argues that most attention
is spent unconsciously — given away in small increments that individually feel
trivial and collectively consume the capacity for any work requiring sustained
depth. The calendar is the external version of the mind's attention. What is
on it reflects what you have agreed to attend to.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

For the first 90 minutes of your working day tomorrow, run a single experiment:
no email, no messaging app, no browser tab opened for any reason other than your
primary task. Before you start, write down the task. After the 90 minutes, write
one sentence: what did you actually produce, and did the work feel different from
your normal working state?

--- THE TAGS ---

[warning] [practice] [internally focused] [evidence-based]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Leroy's attention residue research (2009, Organizational Behavior and Human
Decision Processes) has been replicated with reasonable consistency in workplace
settings and directionally supports Newport's structural claim about cognitive
switching costs. Research on task-switching (Rubinstein, Meyer & Evans, 2001,
Journal of Experimental Psychology) provides additional support. [Qualifier:
Newport's broader claim — that depth produces a meaningfully better life — is
philosophical and exceeds what cognitive switching-cost research demonstrates.
The productivity claim is better supported than the meaning claim.] (Strong
alignment for cognitive switching costs; good directional support for the
productivity argument; speculative application for the meaning argument)

Title: The Account You Run Each Morning
Thinker: Marcus Aurelius
Arc: 1 | Day: 7 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: The people I deal with today
will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous and surly. They are
like this because they cannot tell good from evil. But I have seen the beauty
of good, and the ugliness of evil, and have recognized that the wrongdoer has
a nature related to my own."
(Meditations 2.1, trans. Gregory Hays)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

This is one of the most misread passages in Meditations. It sounds like cynicism
— a preparation for disappointment. It is the opposite. It is a pre-emptive
inoculation against the distraction of moral indignation. Aurelius is not saying
people are bad and he has made peace with it. He is saying: I know what today
will bring; I am not going to be surprised by it; and because I am not
surprised, I will not waste my attention on the reactive loop of being offended,
retaliating, complaining, or avoiding.

The morning review — the Stoic practice of premeditatio malorum, or the
premeditation of adversity — is an attention management tool disguised as a
character exercise. By naming, before they happen, the specific ways the day
will resist you, you defuse their power to capture your focus. The difficult
colleague does not become less difficult. What changes is your cognitive posture
toward them: you have already decided, before the encounter, that their behavior
is not a signal requiring a full response from your inner attention.

This is Aurelius's second appearance in the arc (Day 1 was the retreat; Day 7
is the morning account) because the Meditations returns to the same practices
repeatedly — not because Aurelius discovered them once, but because he needed
to re-establish them daily. The Meditations is not a philosophy text written for
publication. It is a working document of a man practicing philosophy under
sustained pressure. That is its most important editorial fact, and the one most
readers miss.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Tomorrow morning, before you open any device, spend three minutes on the
Aurelius account: write one sentence for each of the following — (1) the
specific friction I will most likely encounter today, (2) the person most likely
to pull my attention off course, (3) the task I am most likely to avoid. Do not
problem-solve any of them. Simply name them. The naming is the practice.

--- THE TAGS ---

[practice] [internally focused] [mindset] [overlap]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Research on implementation intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999, American Psychologist)
— if-then planning that pre-specifies how to respond to anticipated obstacles —
has strong empirical support for increasing goal-directed behavior and reducing
the capture of attention by competing stimuli. The Aurelius morning account is
a version of this practice, anticipating friction before it arrives. [Qualifier:
The Stoic morning review has an ethical dimension — it is not merely a planning
tool but an exercise in cultivating equanimity as a character trait.
Implementation intention research measures goal completion, not character
formation.] (Strong alignment)

Title: The Same Argument, Two Thousand Years Apart
Thinkers: Epictetus + Henry David Thoreau
Arc: 1 | Day: 8 | Prompt Archetype: Audit

--- THE SEED ---

"Seek not that the things which happen should happen as you wish; but wish the
things which happen to be as they are, and you will have a tranquil flow of
life." (Epictetus, Enchiridion 8, trans. Thomas Wentworth Higginson)

"I know of no more encouraging fact than the unquestionable ability of man to
elevate his life by a conscious endeavour."
(Thoreau, Walden, "Where I Lived, and What I Lived For")

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Two men, nineteen centuries apart, working from entirely different philosophical
traditions, reaching the same structural conclusion: the liberation of attention
begins with voluntary limitation. Epictetus limits his desires to what is already
the case — a discipline of wanting only what you can have. Thoreau limits his
obligations to what he has consciously chosen — a discipline of doing only what
he has decided to do. The mechanism differs. The direction is identical.

This is what the [overlap] tag is for. It is not that Epictetus and Thoreau agree
on metaphysics, on religion, on the nature of the self, or on what constitutes
a good society. They disagree significantly on all of those. The overlap is
structural: both discovered, independently, that the expansion of desire and
obligation beyond what is necessary is the primary driver of the attention
problem. The Stoic philosophical apparatus and the Transcendentalist experiment
arrive at the same practical prescription — not because they borrowed from each
other, but because they were looking at the same human condition.

What this convergence implies is worth sitting with. When thinkers from
different traditions, centuries apart, with different biographical contexts and
different philosophical frameworks arrive at the same practical conclusion, it
is a reasonable signal that the conclusion is tracking something real. Not
proven — but worth treating as more than coincidence.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Today, find one thing in your life that you are currently wanting to be different
than it is — a circumstance, a person's behavior, a project's progress. Run both
lenses simultaneously. Epictetus: can you genuinely accept this as it is and
redirect your energy to your response? Thoreau: was this situation one you
consciously chose, or did it accumulate? Write one sentence from each lens. Do
not try to resolve the tension between them — notice which one you find harder
to apply.

--- THE TAGS ---

[overlap] [mindset] [practice] [internally focused]

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

The convergence of independent traditions on a shared practical insight does not
constitute empirical proof but represents what philosophers of science sometimes
call consilience of inductions — independent lines of inquiry converging on a
shared conclusion increases the credibility of that conclusion without
establishing it. No direct empirical citation is warranted here.
[Qualifier: This is a philosophical observation about structural convergence
across traditions, not an evidence-based claim.] (Speculative application)

Title: Two Maps of the Same Territory
Thinkers: Cal Newport + Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi
Arc: 1 | Day: 9 | Prompt Archetype: In-The-Wild

--- THE SEED ---

"Schedule every minute of your day." (Newport, Deep Work, Chapter 4 — direct
prescription for time-block planning)

"The key is not to prioritize what's on your schedule, but to schedule your
priorities." (Csikszentmihalyi — paraphrase of the challenge-skill calibration
principle from Flow; the formulation here is interpretive, not a direct quote)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Newport and Csikszentmihalyi are pointing at the same destination through
different windows, and the view from each window reveals something the other
obscures. Newport's prescription is structural and intentional: decide in
advance what you will attend to, block it explicitly, defend it against the
reactive pull of incoming demands. His underlying assumption is that without
external architecture, attention will be colonized by whatever shouts loudest.
Structure is the precondition for depth.

Csikszentmihalyi's research complicates this in a useful way: the conditions
under which flow actually emerges are not always plannable. Flow requires
challenge calibrated to skill, and that calibration is often discovered mid-task,
not in advance. Some of the most reliable flow experiences his research subjects
reported were not scheduled — they arose from full engagement with work that
demanded it. The structure can provide the space. It cannot guarantee the state.

The [tension] here is real and productive. Newport says: protect the time and
the depth will follow. Csikszentmihalyi says: the depth requires conditions that
structure enables but does not determine. This does not resolve neatly. The
practitioner who only follows Newport risks building a schedule full of
deep-work blocks that feel like obligation rather than engagement. The
practitioner who only follows Csikszentmihalyi risks waiting for the right
conditions and never protecting the time in which they could arrive.

The practical synthesis — and it is one you must construct for yourself — is to
use Newport's structure to create the conditions and Csikszentmihalyi's
phenomenology to evaluate whether the conditions are working. Schedule the
blocks. Then pay attention to whether you are actually in the channel.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Look at your schedule for tomorrow. Identify your one deep-work block. Before
you sit down for it, write one sentence: what is the specific challenge level
of this task for me today, on a rough scale from 1 (too easy) to 10
(overwhelming)? A 7-8 is your target. If it is below 5, you need a harder
version of the problem. If it is above 9, you need to decompose it. Adjust
before you start.

(Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 8.7, trans. Gregory Hays)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Ten days in, the arc's central tension is now visible. You have been carrying
two models of what good attention looks like, and they point in different
directions.

Aurelius's model is disciplined and architectured. The morning account (Day 7),
the 60-second observation (Day 1), the two-column exercise (Day 2) — these are
trained practices. They require deliberate effort. They are built and maintained
through repetition, as a muscle is. The Stoic tradition is explicitly a training
tradition: the philosophical exercises are not insights you have once, they are
practices you do daily precisely because the mind, left unmanaged, defaults to
distraction, resentment, and reactive noise.

Suzuki's model is structurally opposite. Beginner's mind (Day 5) is not achieved
by effort — it is recovered by releasing the effort to be an expert. The Zen
tradition views excessive architectural deliberateness as its own kind of
obstacle: the mind that is strenuously trying to be present is doing something
with attention that presence does not require. Wu wei — effortless action — is
not laziness. It is action so calibrated to the moment that there is no friction
between the actor and the act. You do not force the water to flow downhill.

Neither model is wrong. The tension between them is [unresolved] at the level of
theory. But at the level of practice, you can notice that they address different
problems. Aurelius is writing for a mind that is undisciplined and reactive, one
that needs structure imposed from outside. Suzuki is writing for a mind that
may have become over-structured — too controlled, too deliberate, losing contact
with the natural responsiveness beneath the training. The question the arc now
carries forward is not which model is correct. It is: which problem do you
actually have right now?

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Sit for ten minutes today without an agenda. Not meditation with a technique.
Not journaling. Not the Aurelius morning account. Simply sit and let your
attention go where it goes. When the ten minutes are over, write one word: what
did it want?
