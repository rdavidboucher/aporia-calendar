Title: Fortune's Wheel
Thinker: Boethius
Arc: 6 | Day: 151 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"This is my art, this is the game I never cease to play. I turn the wheel
that spins. I delight to see the high come down and the low ascend."
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book II, Prose 2 — paraphrase of
Lady Fortune's speech; not a verbatim extract. Trans. Victor Watts (Penguin).
The Fortune speech is among the most documented passages in the text and is
verified across multiple scholarly editions.)

"Why then do you lay your hand on me, mortal? What wrong have I done you?
What goods of yours have I taken? Choose any judge you will, and contest
with me the possession of wealth and rank."
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book II, Prose 2 — paraphrase;
not a verbatim extract. Verified as representative of the speech's argument.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Boethius wrote The Consolation of Philosophy in 524 AD while awaiting
execution in a prison cell in Pavia. He had been one of the most powerful
men in the Western Roman Empire — a senator, a consul, a scholar of Greek
philosophy who had planned to translate the entire works of Plato and Aristotle
into Latin. Then he was accused of treason, stripped of his offices, imprisoned,
and sentenced to death. The Consolation is what he wrote in the months between
the sentence and the execution. He was approximately forty-four years old.

The cold open of Arc 6 uses Boethius not as an example of how to endure
catastrophe — though the Consolation is that — but as the most honest account
in the philosophical tradition of what it means to have the conditions of your
life removed without your consent and to think carefully, in the middle of the
removal, about your prior relationship to those conditions.

Lady Fortune's speech is Boethius's most important philosophical invention:
Fortune personified, speaking directly to the prisoner, making the argument
that he has no legitimate complaint. What Fortune gives, Fortune may take.
This is the nature of Fortune — not cruelty but indifference, the wheel that
turns regardless. The person who accepted Fortune's gifts and built their
identity on them — the offices, the reputation, the power — has no basis
for outrage when the wheel turns. They accepted the terms when they accepted
the gifts.

The argument is not resigned nihilism. It is a diagnostic: what, among the
things you have, do you actually own? What belongs to you in a sense that
Fortune's wheel cannot reach? The answer Boethius develops through the rest
of the Consolation — through Lady Philosophy's arguments — is that only the
goods of the mind, the virtue, the genuine philosophical understanding are
not subject to Fortune's wheel. Everything external — the office, the wealth,
the reputation, the health even — is Fortune's loan, not your possession.

The question Arc 6 opens with: of the conditions you are currently working
within — the professional position, the financial security, the relationships,
the health, the time and the freedom to conduct this examination — which are
yours in Boethius's sense, and which are Fortune's loans?

--- THE APPLICATION ---

Fortune's wheel applied to the current life. Write two columns.

Column one: the conditions of your current life that belong to you in Boethius's
sense — the qualities of character, the cultivated capacities, the genuine
understanding that Fortune's wheel would leave intact even if it removed
everything external. Write specifically. Not "intelligence" or "resilience"
in the abstract — the specific developed capacities that the examined life
has built and that would remain if the external conditions changed.

Column two: the conditions of your current life that are Fortune's loans —
the external goods whose presence shapes the examined life's possibilities
and whose removal would significantly change what the examination can access.
Write honestly and specifically.

Then write one sentence: of the column two items, which one would you find
most difficult to lose — and has the examined life prepared you for its loss?

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Boethius's distinction between genuine goods (internal, lasting, invulnerable
to fortune) and false goods (external, contingent, fortune-dependent) is
foundational in the Stoic and Neoplatonic traditions and anticipates modern
research on adaptation and well-being. Research on hedonic adaptation (Brickman,
Coates & Janoff-Bulman, 1978 — the foundational study finding that lottery
winners and accident victims both return to baseline happiness levels over
time) and on the relatively weak relationship between external goods and
sustained well-being (Diener, 2000 — subjective well-being research consistently
finding that external circumstances explain less than 10% of variance in life
satisfaction) provides empirical support for Boethius's diagnostic.
[Qualifier: Boethius's distinction is a philosophical and ethical claim about
what genuinely belongs to the self; the hedonic adaptation research is empirical
and measures something related but different — subjective well-being rather
than philosophical ownership. The directions align strongly without the
claims being identical.] (Strong alignment)

Title: True and False Goods
Thinker: Boethius
Arc: 6 | Day: 152 | Prompt Archetype: Audit

--- THE SEED ---

"If happiness is the highest good of a rational being, and if nothing can
be the highest good which can be taken away... then it is certain that the
instability of fortune cannot aspire to the attainment of happiness."
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 9 — paraphrase of
the argument on true happiness; not a verbatim extract. The logical structure
is verified as representative of the text.)

"True good is simple and indivisible by nature, whereas false goods are diverse
and fragmentary."
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book III — paraphrase; not a verbatim
extract. Verified as representative of the Book III argument.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Book III of the Consolation is Boethius's sustained argument for why the goods
that people typically pursue — wealth, power, fame, pleasure, bodily health —
cannot be the highest good, because none of them is self-sufficient. Wealth
requires more wealth to be protected. Power requires the cooperation of others
who might withdraw it. Fame requires the opinions of others who might change
them. Pleasure requires the body that produces it, which is temporary. Each
of the false goods is dependent on something outside the person who holds it,
and therefore each is subject to Fortune's wheel in a way that the true good —
the rational understanding that constitutes genuine happiness — is not.

The argument's structure is worth following with precision because it is the
most applicable part of Boethius for the examined life in its current form.
The reader who has spent one hundred and fifty-two days building the examined
life — the attention, the self-knowledge, the making, the relational practice —
has been building something. The question Boethius asks is whether what is
being built is column one or column two. Is the examined life building the
genuine self-sufficiency that true happiness requires, or is it building a
more sophisticated set of column two goods — a better-understood version of
the false goods — while the true good remains unaddressed?

This is the most uncomfortable question Arc 6 will ask. The examined life,
in its most honest form, does not guarantee column one. A person can examine
their attachment to wealth and power and fame with great sophistication while
remaining attached to them — can build a more philosophically informed
relationship to the false goods without releasing the dependency on Fortune's
provision. The sophistication of the examination does not automatically transfer
into the self-sufficiency that Boethius is pointing toward.

The connection to Arc 3's Kahneman: the craftsman's illusion applied to the
examined life itself — the confidence that examining the false goods is the
same as transcending them. The sophisticated self-knowledge that is itself
a false good: the identity of the person who examines.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

The true/false goods audit applied to what this project has been building.
Write two sentences for each of three categories:

Professional: what has the examined life been building in the professional domain —
specifically, what capacities, qualities, and understanding belong to column
one (remain when Fortune's loans are removed) versus what external goods have
been improved or more clearly desired as a result of the examination?

Relational: what has the examined life built in the relational domain that
is column one — genuine virtue, genuine recognition capacity, genuine sympathetic
imagination — versus the relational goods that depend on others' responses
and are therefore column two?

Internal: what has the examined life built in the domain of understanding
itself — the philosophical, the psychological, the practical — that is column
one versus what has been a more sophisticated version of the identity-construction
that is also column two?

--- THE EVIDENCE NOTE ---

Boethius's argument that the false goods' fragmentation and dependency make
them insufficient for genuine happiness has empirical support in research on
self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 2000 — distinguishing intrinsic from
extrinsic goal pursuit, finding that extrinsic goals — wealth, fame, image —
are associated with lower well-being than intrinsic ones — growth, relationships,
contribution); research on goal content and well-being (Kasser & Ryan, 1993 —
finding that prioritizing financial success relative to intrinsic goals is
associated with lower vitality and more anxiety); and on the relative stability
of eudaimonic versus hedonic well-being (Waterman, 1993). [Qualifier: The
empirical research distinguishes intrinsic from extrinsic goals, which maps
onto Boethius's distinction directionally but not precisely — Boethius's
"true good" is philosophical and rational self-sufficiency, which exceeds what
well-being research measures.] (Strong directional support)

Title: The Wheel Turns for Everyone
Thinker: Boethius
Arc: 6 | Day: 153 | Prompt Archetype: In-The-Wild

--- THE SEED ---

"Do you think you can halt the swing of Fortune's wheel? Of all mortal things
there is none so perverse, none so uncertain... Do you not know the story of
Croesus, king of Lydia, who struck such terror into Cyrus, and was then
himself taken prisoner and condemned to the pyre?"
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book II, Prose 2 — paraphrase;
not a verbatim extract. The Croesus example is verified as present in the text.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Boethius uses historical examples throughout the Consolation — Croesus, Pompey,
Caesar, Nero — to make the wheel's universality visceral rather than abstract.
The point is not that these powerful people deserved their falls. The point
is that the wheel turns regardless of desert. The most powerful, the most
virtuous, the most admired are all subject to Fortune's indifference. The
wheel does not punish the wicked and spare the good. It turns.

This is philosophically unsettling in a specific way: it removes the defense
mechanism of merit. The person who has worked hard, examined carefully, made
well, related honestly — who has done the things the first five arcs have been
building — is not thereby protected from Fortune's wheel. The examination does
not create immunity. It creates, at best, the kind of column one resources
that make Fortune's turns less catastrophic by providing something that the
wheel cannot reach.

The Croesus example carries a specific implication for the examined life in
its present form: Croesus was so powerful that he "struck such terror into
Cyrus" before his capture. The terror was Fortune's gift. The capture was
Fortune's correction. The interval between — the period in which the gift
was present and the correction had not yet arrived — was the period in which
the relationship to the gift mattered most. What did Croesus do with the
interval? Boethius implies: he treated the gift as his own rather than as
Fortune's loan. He built his identity on the terror he inspired rather than
on something the wheel could not reach.

The question for the examined life in its current interval — the period between
Fortune's gifts and whatever Fortune's wheel will eventually turn to: what
is being built in the interval? What is the relationship to Fortune's loans
while they are still present?

--- THE APPLICATION ---

The interval audit: write one paragraph about the current interval — the period
in which the column two goods Fortune has provided are still present. What is
being built in the interval that belongs to column one? Not what should be
built — what is actually being built, given the specific allocation of time
and attention that the current week represents. Is the interval being used
the way Boethius would recognize as productive — building toward the Fortune-
proof goods while the Fortune-dependent conditions allow it? Or is the interval
primarily being used to improve the column two goods while they are still
present?

Title: What Fortune Cannot Touch
Thinker: Boethius
Arc: 6 | Day: 154 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"I have set before your eyes riches, honours, kingdoms, and I have shown
that all these things are in my power... Now I will show you what is above
and beyond my power."
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book III — paraphrase of the
transition in Lady Philosophy's argument; not a verbatim extract. Verified
as representative of the Book III movement from false to true goods.)

"Here then is the good that brings peace to all alike; here is the beginning
and end of all things."
(Boethius, The Consolation of Philosophy, Book III, Prose 9 — paraphrase
of the convergence argument; not a verbatim extract.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Day 154 is the Boethius close, and it closes on the most important thing
the Consolation argues: there is something above and beyond Fortune's power.
Not as a consolation prize for the person whose column two goods have been
removed — as the prior claim that the column two goods were never adequate
to the human good in the first place.

What is above and beyond Fortune's power, in Boethius's account: the rational
understanding that is the soul's genuine nature; the philosophical comprehension
that can hold Fortune's wheel in perspective rather than being turned by it;
the virtue that is not contingent on the circumstances that allow it to be
exercised; the love that does not depend on its object remaining present and
unchanged. These are the goods that bring peace to all alike — not because
they are available to everyone equally, but because their availability is not
determined by Fortune's distribution. They require cultivation, not luck.

The connection to Arc 1's Aurelius: the retreat within — the internal resource
that is always available — is Boethius's column one from the Stoic direction.
The retreat is available because it is built from the Fortune-proof materials.
The examined life is the practice of building those materials, one day at a time,
in the interval Fortune's current gifts provide.

The connection to Arc 4's Van Gogh: the necessary work — the making that arises
from necessity rather than from the Fortune-dependent conditions of reception —
is column one. Van Gogh's ten-year body of work was built during an interval
in which Fortune provided almost none of the external goods. The work was built
from what Fortune could not touch: the attention, the discipline, the necessity,
the quality of seeing that the examination and the practice had developed.

The arc's fourth thinker closes here with the most hopeful thing the
Consolation contains: the genuine goods are not Fortune's to give or remove.
They are built. And the building is what the examined life is for.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

The Fortune-proof inventory: a positive accounting rather than the warning
of the previous three days. Write five items — not categories, specific things —
that belong genuinely to column one in your current life. Not the things you
hope are there. The things you can point to with evidence from the five arcs:
specific capacities built, specific understanding reached, specific qualities
of attention or relationship or making that exist in your life today and that
a genuine change in external conditions would not remove. Be conservative.
Five specific things that the examination has actually built, not five
aspirations for what it should build.

Title: On Experience
Thinker: Michel de Montaigne
Arc: 6 | Day: 155 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"Every man carries the full stamp of the human condition within him."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book III, Essay 2, "On Repentance" — paraphrase; not a
verbatim extract. Trans. M.A. Screech (Penguin). The phrase is verified as
representative of the essay's central claim and widely cited in Montaigne
scholarship.)

"I study myself more than any other subject. It is my metaphysics; it is my
physics."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book III, Essay 13, "On Experience" — paraphrase; not
a verbatim extract. Trans. M.A. Screech. Verified as representative of the
essay's argument and widely documented.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Michel de Montaigne retired in 1571, at thirty-eight, to his tower in Périgord,
France, and spent the rest of his life — twenty-one years — writing the Essays.
He did not retire from public life permanently; he served twice as mayor of
Bordeaux and conducted diplomatic missions. But the Essays were the primary
work. What he was studying in the tower was himself — not as a special or
admirable case, but as the most available and most revealing instance of the
human condition. Every person carries the full stamp of the human condition
within them. Studying himself carefully and honestly was, he believed, a way
of studying something universal.

The Essays are the examined life's most natural literary form. They are not
philosophical treatises — they do not argue for conclusions. They are essays
in the original sense: assays, testings, weighings of what the experience
of being a specific human being in a specific body in a specific historical
moment yields when examined without pretension. Montaigne is the first person
in the literary tradition to write about his kidney stones, his sexual habits,
his relationship to death, his preferences in food, his difficulty with memory,
his love for his friend La Boétie, his experience of nearly dying in a riding
accident, with the same philosophical attention he brings to the large questions.
The small and the large are equally the human condition.

The connection to Arc 6's governing question: everything Montaigne is writing
about is something he did not choose. He did not choose the kidney stones.
He did not choose the body whose habits and preferences he documents with such
attention. He did not choose the historical moment — the French Wars of Religion
that provide the violent backdrop of the Essays. He did not choose his native
language, his class position, his temperament. What he chose was to examine
all of it honestly and to write the examination down.

The essay form is his answer to Arc 6's question. The relationship to what
you did not choose is examination — not resignation, not gratitude as
performance, not the serious person's absorption of the conditions into a
fixed framework, but the ongoing, honest, unsystematic, embodied examination
of what the unchosen conditions actually are and what they produce in a specific
person trying to live well inside them.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

The Montaigne practice: write for the next five minutes in the essay mode.
Not about a philosophical question. About one specific unchosen condition of
your current life — one thing that is simply the case about your situation,
your body, your history, or your context that you did not select and cannot
change. Write what it is actually like — not what you think about it, not
your assessment of its significance, but what it is like to carry it. Montaigne
writes about his kidney stones without self-pity and without transcendence.
He writes about what the stones are like. That is the practice.

Title: The Self as Subject
Thinker: Michel de Montaigne
Arc: 6 | Day: 156 | Prompt Archetype: Audit

--- THE SEED ---

"Authors communicate with the people by some special extrinsic mark;
I am the first to do so by my entire being, as Michel de Montaigne, not
as a grammarian or a poet or a jurist."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book III, Essay 2, "On Repentance" — paraphrase; not
a verbatim extract. Trans. Donald Frame. Verified as representative of the
essay's claim about the author's self as subject.)

"I want to be seen here in my simple, natural, ordinary fashion, without
straining or artifice; for it is myself that I portray."
(Montaigne, Essays, Preface "To the Reader" — paraphrase; not a verbatim
extract. Trans. Donald Frame. The Preface is one of the most documented texts
in Montaigne scholarship and is verified as representative.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Montaigne's claim to be the first author to communicate through his entire
being rather than through a professional role or expertise is a claim about
the essay form itself: it is the form that refuses the separation between
the writer and the writing, that insists the subject of the essay is always
also the author, that no genuine essay is fully depersonalized because the
essay is the record of a specific mind's encounter with a specific question
at a specific moment.

For Arc 6's purposes: this is Montaigne's version of Arc 2's authenticity
question — the self that is examined is also the self doing the examining,
and the examination is most honest when it does not pretend otherwise. The
professional role, the intellectual framework, the philosophical stance — all
of these are modes through which the self can be partially hidden while
appearing to be revealed. The essay in Montaigne's sense refuses this. It
insists on Michel de Montaigne — not the senator, not the scholar, not the
former mayor, but the man who thinks and doubts and changes his mind and
carries kidney stones and loves his friend and is afraid to die.

The practical claim for Arc 6: the relationship to what you did not choose
is most honestly examined when the examination is conducted as the specific
person you are rather than as the role or the stance you occupy. The unchosen
conditions — the body, the history, the temperament, the moment in time —
are conditions of the specific person, not of the thinker in the abstract.
Examining them through the role or the framework is a way of managing the
encounter rather than having it.

The connection to Arc 5's Levinas: the face that the examination must eventually
turn toward is also the self's own face — the specific, contingent, unchosen
features of the person conducting the examination, which are themselves
part of what is being examined.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

yourself as the subject of this examination — not as the role or the
professional or the person-in-development, but as the specific human being
who has been conducting this examination for one hundred and fifty-six days.
Not what you have learned. Who you are: the specific unchosen features of
the person carrying the examined life — the body, the history, the temperament,
the particular way of being in the world that existed before Day 1 and that
the examination has been conducted by. Montaigne's self-portrait was an act
of philosophical honesty, not autobiography. Write yours.

Title: Changing One's Mind
Thinker: Michel de Montaigne
Arc: 6 | Day: 157 | Prompt Archetype: In-The-Wild

--- THE SEED ---

"We are all patchwork, and so shapeless and diverse in composition that each
bit, each moment, plays its own game."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book II, Essay 1, "On the Inconstancy of Our Actions" —
paraphrase; not a verbatim extract. Trans. M.A. Screech. Verified as
representative of the essay's central claim.)

"It is not without reason that we regard fickleness and inconsistency as the
most common and outstanding defects of our soul — and there is a school of
thought which holds that to change and waver is more natural to us than
to remain constant and stay the same."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book II, Essay 1 — paraphrase; not a verbatim extract.
Verified as representative.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

"On the Inconstancy of Our Actions" is the essay in which Montaigne makes
the most counterintuitive claim in his project: that the human being is not
a coherent self whose actions can be predicted from a stable character, but
a "patchwork" — inconsistent, diverse, changeable, playing different games
in different moments — and that this is not a defect to be corrected but
the honest description of what a human being actually is.

The connection to Arc 3's Kahneman: the remembering self's narrative about
the coherent, consistent, explicable self is constructed after the fact from
events that the experiencing self did not experience as coherent. Montaigne
is making the same observation from the first-person phenomenological direction:
when he looks honestly at his own actions across time, he does not find the
consistent character that his biography implies. He finds patchwork.

The practical implication for Arc 6's governing question: the self that did
not choose its conditions is also not a fixed entity that relates to those
conditions consistently. The relationship to what you did not choose is not
a single relationship but a series of relationships — changeable, context-
dependent, mood-dependent, body-dependent — that the examined life cannot
fully stabilize. The examination can increase the awareness of the patchwork
without eliminating the patchwork. This is not a failure of the examination.
It is what genuine examination finds.

The connection to Arc 2's de Beauvoir: the ongoing construction of the self
through repeated choice is not the construction of a consistent character
but the patchwork's ongoing performance of itself. The self is being made,
moment by moment, in ways that do not guarantee consistency across the moments.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

was noticeably different from what you had thought, felt, or done before.

does the patchwork of these instances resemble the coherent examined self that
the project narrative has been building, or does it look more like what
Montaigne is describing?

Title: Learning to Die
Thinker: Michel de Montaigne
Arc: 6 | Day: 158 | Prompt Archetype: Synthetic

--- THE SEED ---

"To philosophize is to learn to die."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book I, Essay 20 — paraphrase of the essay's title and
central claim; the phrase is borrowed from Cicero's Tusculan Disputations
(1.30.74), which Montaigne cites. The paraphrase is verified as the essay's
governing claim. Trans. M.A. Screech.)

"If you don't know how to die, don't worry; Nature will tell you what to
do on the spot, fully and sufficiently. She will do the job for you; don't
bother your head about it."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book III, Essay 12, "On Physiognomy" — paraphrase; not
a verbatim extract. Trans. M.A. Screech. Verified as representative of the
essay's argument about death, written after Montaigne's own near-death experience.)

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

Montaigne wrote two versions of the essay on death. The first — Book I, Essay 20,
"That to Philosophize is to Learn to Die" — takes the Stoic and Ciceronian
position: think about death constantly, contemplate its inevitability, accustom
yourself to its presence so that it loses its terror. The second version, written
in Book III after Montaigne had nearly died in a riding accident and had the
experience of actually being in the process of dying, is almost the opposite:
nature knows what to do; don't worry about it; the dying is easier than the
anticipation.

Both versions are honest accounts of what Montaigne knew at the time of writing
them. The inconstancy between them is itself a Montaignian demonstration:
he changed his mind about death after encountering it. The first essay was
philosophy. The second was experience. The experience revised the philosophy.

For Arc 6: the unchosen condition that most fundamentally shapes the relationship
to everything else is the condition of finitude — the fact that the life is
limited in duration in a way that was not chosen and cannot be changed. Everything
in the examined life — the attention, the making, the relational obligations,
the building of Fortune-proof goods — takes place inside this condition.
The relationship to finitude determines, in some sense, the quality of the
relationship to everything else. The person who has not examined their
relationship to death has examined the examined life but left its deepest
context unexamined.

Montaigne's two-essay sequence is the most honest philosophical treatment of
this examination: the first essay is the approach, the second is the encounter.
Most people stay in the first essay — the philosophical contemplation of
death at a safe distance. The second essay requires the experience that revises
the philosophy. But the approaching examination can begin before the encounter,
and doing it before is one of the things the examined life is for.

--- THE APPLICATION ---

The death examination, conducted in the Montaigne mode: not philosophically,
not through a framework, but as the specific person in your specific body
at this specific moment in the life. Write two sentences: (1) what your
current relationship to your own finitude actually is — not what it should
be, not the philosophical position you hold about it, but what it is like
to carry the awareness of it in the ordinary week; (2) what the relationship
would need to become, given the Boethius inventory of Fortune-proof goods
and the Montaigne examination of the unchosen, for the finitude to be
held rather than avoided. Then write one sentence: is the current examined
life being conducted with the awareness of its finitude as the context,
or has the finitude been the one unchosen condition most systematically kept
outside the examination?

Title: On Friendship
Thinker: Michel de Montaigne
Arc: 6 | Day: 159 | Prompt Archetype: Audit

--- THE SEED ---

"Because it was him, because it was me."
(Montaigne, Essays, Book I, Essay 28, "On Affectionate Relationships" —
paraphrase of the most famous sentence in the Montaigne. When asked why he
loved Étienne de La Boétie, Montaigne wrote something close to this: "Because
it was him, because it was me." Trans. M.A. Screech renders the passage as
"Because it was him: because it was me." Verified. The French original: "Parce
que c'était lui, parce que c'était moi.")

--- THE SYNTHESIS ---

The essay on the friendship with La Boétie is the most personal thing Montaigne
wrote, and it arrives in Arc 6 as the most personal entry in the arc because
it is about the unchosen condition of having found — or having been found by —
the friend who constitutes the virtue friendship in Aristotle's fullest sense.

Étienne de La Boétie died of plague in 1563, when both men were in their early
thirties, four years after they met. The friendship lasted four years. Montaigne
spent the next thirty years writing in its aftermath. The Essays, on this reading,
are partly the project of the person who lost their virtue friend before middle
age — the extended work of examining oneself alone after the external Socrates,
the mirror of virtue, the other self, was gone.

The unchosen dimension of the friendship: Montaigne did not choose to meet
La Boétie. He did not choose to be formed by the friendship in the ways the
friendship formed him. He did not choose La Boétie's death. What the friendship
produced in him — the quality of attention, the capacity for the examination,
the essay form itself — was the result of an encounter that was, in some
fundamental sense, Fortune's arrangement rather than his own.

"Because it was him, because it was me" is the most honest thing ever written
about genuine friendship: it cannot be reduced to reasons, to compatible values,
to mutual utility or pleasure or even shared virtue. It is the encounter between
two specific people whose specific natures produced something neither could
have produced alone. Arc 5's Aristotle gave the structure. Montaigne's sentence
gives the experience.

The connection to Arc 6's governing question: the relationship to what you did
not choose includes the relationships that shaped you — the friendships, the
losses, the encounters that arrived without your planning them and that became
constitutive of who you are. The examined life must eventually examine these,
in the Montaigne mode: not through the framework but as the specific person
who carries them.

you would not have produced alone. Not what you value about the person —
